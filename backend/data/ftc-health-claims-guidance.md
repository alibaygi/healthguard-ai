# FTC Health Products Compliance Guidance — Key Rules for Social Media Content

## Source: Federal Trade Commission (FTC), December 2022
## Document: Health Products Compliance Guidance (formerly "Dietary Supplements: An Advertising Guide for Industry")

---

## SECTION 1: CORE PRINCIPLES

### Rule 1.1 — Truthfulness Requirement
All advertising for health-related products must be truthful and not misleading. This applies to ALL forms of marketing including social media posts, TikTok videos, Instagram reels, YouTube videos, influencer content, and any digital promotion.

### Rule 1.2 — Substantiation Requirement
Before disseminating any ad or promotional content, advertisers MUST have adequate substantiation for ALL objective product claims conveyed, expressly or by implication, to consumers acting reasonably. Claims about health benefits or safety require substantiation in the form of "competent and reliable scientific evidence."

### Rule 1.3 — Competent and Reliable Scientific Evidence Standard
The FTC defines this as: "tests, analyses, research, or studies that (1) have been conducted and evaluated in an objective manner by experts in the relevant disease, condition, or function to which the representation relates; and (2) are generally accepted in the profession to yield accurate and reliable results."

### Rule 1.4 — Universal Applicability
These rules apply equally to:
- Traditional TV, radio, and print advertising
- Social media posts (TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, Facebook, Twitter/X)
- Influencer marketing and sponsored content
- Blog posts, podcasts, and digital content
- Product packaging and labeling
- Press releases and media appearances

## SECTION 2: IDENTIFYING HEALTH CLAIMS

### Rule 2.1 — Express vs. Implied Claims
Marketers are responsible for BOTH express claims (directly stated) and implied claims (suggested or reasonably inferred by consumers). You cannot suggest benefits indirectly that you couldn't claim directly.

### Rule 2.2 — Net Impression Standard
The FTC evaluates the "net impression" conveyed by ALL elements of an ad, including text, product name, images, charts, graphs, testimonials, and context. Individual phrases are not viewed in isolation.

### Rule 2.3 — Audience-Specific Interpretation
Claims are interpreted from the standpoint of the intended audience. Terminally ill consumers, elderly people, or those with chronic conditions may be particularly susceptible to exaggerated claims.

### Rule 2.4 — Structure/Function Claims That Imply Disease Benefits
A statement about a product's effect on "structure or function" of the body may also convey an implied disease claim. Example: "boosts immune system to help maintain healthy nose and throat during winter" + product name "Cold Away" + images of sneezing → implies cold prevention claim.

### VIOLATION EXAMPLES:
- Images of doctors in white lab coats with medical journals → implies clinical proof
- Before-and-after images showing dramatic health improvements → implies treatment efficacy
- Product named "Arthricure" claiming "maintains joint health" → implies arthritis cure
- "90% of cardiologists take this product" → implies heart health benefit

## SECTION 3: MISLEADING HEALTH CLAIMS ON SOCIAL MEDIA

### Rule 3.1 — Absolute/Guaranteed Health Claims Are Prohibited
Content creators MUST NOT make absolute claims about health products unless backed by competent and reliable scientific evidence. Prohibited language includes:
- "Guaranteed to cure/treat/heal..."
- "Proven to work for everyone"
- "100% effective"
- "Clinically proven" (without actual clinical proof)
- "Doctor approved" (without genuine medical endorsement)

### Rule 3.2 — Weight Loss Claims
Weight loss claims require randomized, controlled human clinical trials (RCTs). The following are violations:
- "Lose 20 pounds in 2 weeks with this supplement"
- "This tea will melt your belly fat overnight"
- "Take this pill and eat whatever you want"
- Before-and-after photos without disclosing diet/exercise regimen
- Testimonials showing dramatic weight loss without typical results disclosure

### Rule 3.3 — Supplement and Vitamin Claims
Dietary supplement claims must be substantiated. Common violations include:
- "This vitamin cures depression/anxiety"
- "Natural immunity booster — prevents COVID/flu/colds"
- "Detox your liver with this supplement"
- "Ancient remedy proven to cure [disease]"
- "This superfood reverses aging"

### Rule 3.4 — "Detox" and "Cleanse" Claims
Most "detox" and "cleanse" products lack scientific evidence. Claims like "flush toxins from your body," "cleanse your organs," or "reset your metabolism" are almost always unsubstantiated and potentially deceptive.

### Rule 3.5 — Anti-Aging Claims
Claims that products can "reverse aging," "eliminate wrinkles," "restore youth," or provide dramatic anti-aging effects require clinical evidence. Most anti-aging product claims on social media lack adequate substantiation.

## SECTION 4: SUBSTANTIATION REQUIREMENTS

### Rule 4.1 — Randomized Controlled Trials Required
As a general matter, substantiation of health-related benefits requires randomized, controlled human clinical testing (RCTs). This is the gold standard.

### Rule 4.2 — Insufficient Evidence Types
The following types of evidence are NOT sufficient to substantiate health claims:
- **Animal studies**: Limited value in predicting effects in humans
- **In vitro studies**: Cannot predict benefits for humans
- **Anecdotal evidence**: Customer testimonials, surveys, individual experiences
- **Traditional use**: Historical use alone doesn't prove efficacy
- **Public health recommendations**: Not equivalent to causal proof
- **Customer satisfaction surveys**: Collection of anecdotes, not scientific proof

### Rule 4.3 — Quality Standards for Clinical Evidence
Valid clinical studies must have:
- Treatment group AND control group (ideally placebo-controlled)
- Appropriate randomization
- Double-blinding (both participants and researchers)
- Statistically significant results between groups
- Clinically meaningful results (not just statistically significant)
- Sufficient duration and follow-up period
- Pre-registered protocol and outcome measures

### Rule 4.4 — Post Hoc Analysis Warning
Post hoc analysis (departing from original study protocol) may indicate data mining or "p-hacking." Multiple post hoc comparisons increase the likelihood of false positive results. Such analysis cannot substantiate health claims on its own.

## SECTION 5: ENDORSEMENTS AND TESTIMONIALS

### Rule 5.1 — Testimonial Substantiation
Consumer testimonials and expert endorsements cannot make claims that would be deceptive or unsubstantiated if the advertiser made them directly. Individual experiences are not a substitute for scientific evidence.

### Rule 5.2 — "Results Not Typical" Is Insufficient
Dramatic testimonial results with disclaimers like "Results not typical" or "Individual results may vary" do NOT cure deception. Advertisers must disclose the results a typical consumer can actually expect.

### Rule 5.3 — Influencer Disclosure Requirements
Any material connection between an endorser (influencer) and the advertiser MUST be clearly and conspicuously disclosed. This includes:
- Paid partnerships
- Free products received
- Affiliate commissions
- Employment relationships
- Family or personal connections

### Rule 5.4 — Expert Endorser Qualifications
Expert endorsers must have:
- Appropriate qualifications in the relevant field
- Conducted examination or testing of the product
- Disclosed any material connection to the advertiser

### VIOLATION EXAMPLES:
- Influencer claims supplement "cured my acid reflux" without scientific backing
- Paid blogger review without disclosing payment
- "Doctor" endorser who has a psychology degree, not medical degree
- Testimonials showing 16-pound weight loss when studies show 4-pound average

## SECTION 6: DISCLOSURE REQUIREMENTS

### Rule 6.1 — Clear and Conspicuous Disclosures
Qualifying information must be:
- Easily noticeable (difficult to miss)
- Easily understandable by ordinary consumers
- In the same medium as the claim (visual + audible if claim is both)
- Not contradicted by other elements of the ad
- Unavoidable in digital media (hyperlinked disclosures are avoidable and insufficient)

### Rule 6.2 — Disclosures Cannot Contradict Claims
A disclosure that directly contradicts an express claim is ineffective. Example: Claiming "treats acne" followed by "for entertainment purposes only" — the disclaimer doesn't negate the treatment claim.

### Rule 6.3 — Vague Qualifiers Are Inadequate
Terms like "may," "helps," "promising," "preliminary," "initial," or "pilot" are NOT adequate disclaimers. Consumers interpret these as positive attributes rather than substantive limitations.

### Rule 6.4 — Side Effects and Safety Risks
Advertisers MUST disclose:
- Known side effects of the product
- Drug interactions (even if no safety claims are made)
- Limitations on who should use the product
- Risks from customary use

## SECTION 7: SPECIFIC HEALTH CATEGORY RULES

### Rule 7.1 — Disease Treatment/Prevention Claims
Claims that a product treats, cures, or prevents ANY disease require the highest level of scientific substantiation. Traditional use claims about treating serious diseases are prohibited even with disclaimers.

### Rule 7.2 — Mental Health Claims
Claims about treating depression, anxiety, ADHD, PTSD, or other mental health conditions require clinical evidence. "Boosts mood," "reduces stress," or "improves focus" may imply mental health treatment claims.

### Rule 7.3 — Cancer Claims
NO health product may claim to treat, cure, or prevent cancer without FDA-approved clinical evidence. Traditional/folk remedy claims for cancer are prohibited even with disclaimers about lack of scientific support.

### Rule 7.4 — Immune System Claims
"Boosts immunity," "strengthens immune system," or "natural immune support" claims must be substantiated. These often imply disease prevention (colds, flu, infections) which requires clinical evidence.

### Rule 7.5 — Blood Sugar/Diabetes Claims
Claims about managing blood sugar, treating diabetes, or improving insulin sensitivity are disease claims requiring clinical evidence.

### Rule 7.6 — Heart Health/Cardiovascular Claims
Claims about reducing heart disease risk, lowering cholesterol, improving blood pressure, or promoting cardiovascular health require clinical substantiation.

### Rule 7.7 — Sleep and Insomnia Claims
Claims that products treat insomnia, improve sleep quality, or act as sleep aids must be substantiated with clinical evidence. Testimonials from satisfied customers are not substantiation.

### Rule 7.8 — Pain Relief Claims
Claims about treating pain (joint pain, headaches, chronic pain, inflammation) require well-controlled clinical studies.

## SECTION 8: SOCIAL MEDIA SPECIFIC GUIDELINES

### Rule 8.1 — TikTok/Short-Form Video Rules
Health claims in short-form video (TikTok, Reels, Shorts) are subject to the SAME substantiation requirements as any other medium. The brief format does NOT exempt creators from:
- Providing adequate disclosures
- Having scientific substantiation
- Disclosing material connections
- Avoiding deceptive implied claims

### Rule 8.2 — Hashtags and Sponsored Content
Using hashtags like #ad, #sponsored, #gifted is necessary but may not be sufficient if the disclosure is not clear and conspicuous. Hashtags buried among dozens of others may not be effective.

### Rule 8.3 — Link-in-Bio Disclaimers
Disclosures placed only in a "link in bio" page are NOT clear and conspicuous. Required disclosures must appear in the content itself, not behind additional clicks.

### Rule 8.4 — Comments and Reply Disclosures
Placing disclaimers only in comments or replies is insufficient. Disclosures must be in the original content.

### Rule 8.5 — Platform-Native Shopping Features
Health claims made through TikTok Shop, Instagram Shopping, or similar platform features are still advertising and must comply with FTC law.

## SECTION 9: FDA DISCLAIMER MISUSE

### Rule 9.1 — DSHEA Disclaimer Limitations
The standard disclaimer "These statements have not been evaluated by the FDA. This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease" does NOT:
- Cure an otherwise deceptive ad
- Allow marketers to make unsubstantiated health claims
- Override express or implied disease treatment claims
- Substitute for scientific substantiation

### Rule 9.2 — FDA Approval Misrepresentation
Advertisers MUST NOT mischaracterize FDA review, authorization, or approval. Compliance with DSHEA notification does not constitute FDA authorization.

## SECTION 10: ENFORCEMENT AND CONSEQUENCES

### Rule 10.1 — Liable Parties
ALL parties involved in deceptive marketing may be liable:
- Product marketers and companies
- Individual owners and corporate officers
- Ad agencies and content creators
- Influencers and endorsers
- Distributors, retailers, and platform sellers
- Infomercial/content producers

### Rule 10.2 — Penalties
The FTC can seek:
- Cease-and-desist orders
- Corrective advertising requirements
- Consumer refunds
- Civil penalties
- Marketing activity bans (in egregious cases)

### Rule 10.3 — Financial Consequence
Companies have been required to pay millions in refunds and penalties for deceptive health advertising. Individual liability extends to company officers and participating influencers.
