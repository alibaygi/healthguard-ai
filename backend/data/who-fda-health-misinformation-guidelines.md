## Sources:
## - World Health Organization (WHO) — Countering Health Misinformation (2024)
## - FDA — Dietary Supplements: What You Need To Know

## SECTION 1: CATEGORIES OF HEALTH MISINFORMATION

### Category 1.1 — Miracle Cure Claims
Any claim that a product, food, supplement, or practice can "cure" a disease or condition without established scientific evidence. Common in TikTok/social media:
- "This cures cancer naturally"
- "Reverse diabetes with this one trick"
- "This herb eliminates all inflammation"
- "Cure your autoimmune disease with diet alone"

**WHY DANGEROUS**: May cause patients to delay or abandon proven medical treatments.

### Category 1.2 — Unsubstantiated Weight Loss Claims
Claims of rapid, effortless, or guaranteed weight loss. Red flags include:
- "Lose 30 pounds in 30 days"
- "No diet or exercise needed"
- "Burns fat while you sleep"
- "Eat whatever you want and still lose weight"
- "This one food melts belly fat"
- Before-and-after transformations without context
- "Metabolism reset" or "metabolic breakthrough"

**WHY DANGEROUS**: Creates unrealistic expectations, promotes disordered eating, and may lead to use of dangerous unregulated products.

### Category 1.3 — Anti-Vaccine / Anti-Medicine Content
Claims that undermine confidence in proven medical treatments:
- "Vaccines cause [disease]"
- "Big Pharma is hiding the cure"
- "Doctors don't want you to know this"
- "Natural is always better than pharmaceutical"
- "Your body can heal anything without medicine"

**WHY DANGEROUS**: Leads to vaccine hesitancy, treatment abandonment, and preventable disease outbreaks.

### Category 1.4 — Pseudoscientific "Detox" Claims
Claims about removing "toxins" from the body through products or practices:
- "Detox your liver/kidneys/blood"
- "Cleanse heavy metals from your body"
- "Flush out all toxins in 7 days"
- "Parasite cleanses" without medical diagnosis
- "Alkaline water cures disease by changing body pH"

**WHY DANGEROUS**: The body has natural detoxification systems (liver, kidneys). Most "detox" products are unproven and may cause harm (dehydration, electrolyte imbalances, dangerous drug interactions).

### Category 1.5 — DIY Medical Treatment
Social media content encouraging self-treatment of serious conditions:
- "How I treated my own [serious condition] at home"
- "Skip the doctor — try this instead"
- "Natural alternatives to [prescription medication]"
- "Stop taking your medications and try this"
- Home remedies presented as replacements for medical care

**WHY DANGEROUS**: Delays proper medical treatment, can cause direct harm from unsafe practices, and may worsen underlying conditions.

## SECTION 2: WHO GUIDELINES ON HEALTH CLAIMS IN FOOD/SUPPLEMENT MARKETING

### Rule 2.1 — Nutrition Claims Must Be Verifiable
Any claim about the nutritional properties of a food or supplement must be based on accepted scientific evidence. Claims must be verifiable and not misleading.

### Rule 2.2 — Health Claims Require Regulatory Authorization
In most jurisdictions, health claims linking a food/ingredient to a health condition require pre-market authorization from relevant regulatory bodies.

### Rule 2.3 — Prohibited Health Claims (Codex Alimentarius)
The following types of claims are generally prohibited:
- Claims that suggest a food can prevent, treat, or cure any disease
- Claims implying that a balanced diet cannot provide adequate nutrition
- Claims that could cause fear or exploit anxiety about health
- Claims referring to advice of health professionals unless substantiated

### Rule 2.4 — Nutrient Function Claims
Claims about the physiological role of nutrients ("Calcium is needed for strong bones") are permitted ONLY when:
- Based on established, well-accepted science
- The nutrient is present in significant amounts
- The claim does not imply disease prevention/treatment

## SECTION 3: FDA RULES ON DIETARY SUPPLEMENTS

### Rule 3.1 — Supplements Are NOT Drugs
Dietary supplements are NOT approved by the FDA to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease. Manufacturers are responsible for ensuring safety and truthful labeling.

### Rule 3.2 — Structure/Function Claims Only
Supplements may make structure/function claims ("supports healthy digestion") but CANNOT make disease claims ("treats Crohn's disease").

### Rule 3.3 — Required Disclaimer
All structure/function claims MUST include: "This statement has not been evaluated by the Food and Drug Administration. This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease."

### Rule 3.4 — Good Manufacturing Practices (GMP)
Supplement manufacturers must follow FDA's GMP requirements, but the FDA does NOT test supplements before they go to market.

### Rule 3.5 — Adverse Event Reporting
Manufacturers must report serious adverse events to the FDA. Content creators promoting supplements should be aware of any reported adverse events.

## SECTION 4: SPECIFIC HEALTH MISINFORMATION RED FLAGS

### Red Flag 4.1 — Emotional Manipulation
Content that uses fear, shame, or anxiety to promote health products:
- "You're slowly poisoning yourself with [common food]"
- "Big Pharma doesn't want you to see this"
- Dramatic scare tactics about common ingredients
- Shaming people for using conventional medicine

### Red Flag 4.2 — Cherry-Picked Science
Presenting isolated or misinterpreted studies as definitive proof:
- Citing a single animal study as proof of human benefits
- Referencing "studies show" without identifying specific research
- Extrapolating lab results (in vitro) to whole-body effects
- Ignoring contradictory evidence from better-designed studies
- Citing retracted or debunked studies

### Red Flag 4.3 — False Authority
Using credentials or authority claims to lend credibility:
- "As a [unrelated credential], I recommend..."
- "Doctors HATE this one trick"
- Wearing lab coats or stethoscopes for visual authority
- Citing credentials in unrelated fields
- Self-proclaimed "health experts" without medical training

### Red Flag 4.4 — Urgency and Scarcity Tactics
Creating artificial urgency around health products:
- "Limited supply — get it before it sells out"
- "Your health can't wait"
- "This offer expires tonight"
- "Everyone is buying this — don't miss out"

### Red Flag 4.5 — Conspiracy Framing
Framing pseudoscience as suppressed truth:
- "What THEY don't want you to know"
- "The medical establishment is hiding this"
- "Censored health information"
- "Banned in [country] because it works too well"

## SECTION 5: SOCIAL MEDIA HEALTH CONTENT BEST PRACTICES

### Best Practice 5.1 — Evidence-Based Content
Health content creators should:
- Cite specific, peer-reviewed studies (not just "studies show")
- Distinguish between correlation and causation
- Acknowledge limitations of cited evidence
- Recommend consulting healthcare providers
- Avoid absolute language ("always," "never," "guaranteed")

### Best Practice 5.2 — Appropriate Disclaimers
Content should include relevant disclaimers:
- "This is not medical advice — consult your healthcare provider"
- "Individual results may vary"
- Disclose any financial relationships with product companies
- Acknowledge when discussing personal experience vs. scientific evidence

### Best Practice 5.3 — Scope of Practice
Content creators should stay within their expertise:
- Licensed nutritionists can discuss nutrition
- Personal trainers can discuss exercise
- Only licensed medical professionals should discuss disease treatment
- Refer viewers to appropriate specialists for specific conditions

### Best Practice 5.4 — Harm Reduction
Content should avoid promoting:
- Extreme caloric restriction or fasting without medical supervision
- Unregulated substances or importing pharmaceuticals
- Discontinuing prescribed medications
- Children's use of adult supplements
- Mega-dosing vitamins or minerals beyond safe upper limits

## SECTION 6: SPECIFIC TRENDING HEALTH MISINFORMATION TOPICS (2024-2026)

### Topic 6.1 — "Raw" and Unpasteurized Products
Claims that raw milk, raw water, or unpasteurized products are healthier. CDC and FDA warn about serious risks including E. coli, Salmonella, and Listeria infections.

### Topic 6.2 — Seed Oil Panic
Claims that seed oils (canola, sunflower, soybean) are "toxic" or cause inflammation. Major nutrition organizations confirm these oils are safe when used as part of a balanced diet.

### Topic 6.3 — Hormone "Optimization"
Promoting testosterone, HGH, or other hormone supplements/injections without medical supervision. Self-prescribed hormone therapy carries serious cardiovascular and metabolic risks.

### Topic 6.4 — "Medical Medium" and Celery Juice
Claims that celery juice or other single foods can cure chronic diseases. No single food can cure disease; these claims lack scientific evidence.

### Topic 6.5 — Turpentine/Borax/MMS Ingestion
Promoting consumption of industrial chemicals as health treatments. These substances are POISONOUS and can cause severe organ damage or death.

### Topic 6.6 — "Natural" Cancer Treatments
Claims that essential oils, alkaline diets, apricot seeds (B17/laetrile), or other natural products can cure cancer. Delaying proven cancer treatment significantly worsens outcomes.

### Topic 6.7 — Social Media "Diagnoses"
TikTok trends where creators diagnose viewers with conditions (ADHD, autism, POTS, EDS) based on vague symptoms. Self-diagnosis without professional evaluation can lead to incorrect treatment.

### Topic 6.8 — Ozempic/GLP-1 Misinformation
Misinformation about diabetes medications being used for weight loss:
- "Natural Ozempic alternatives" (berberine is NOT equivalent)
- Counterfeit or unregulated compound pharmacy versions
- Minimizing side effects or contraindications
- Promoting use without medical supervision

## SECTION 7: SEVERITY CLASSIFICATION

### CRITICAL Violations
- Direct disease cure/treatment claims without evidence
- Promotion of dangerous substances for ingestion
- Advising people to stop prescribed medications
- Making cancer cure claims
- Promoting unproven treatments for life-threatening conditions
- Content targeting vulnerable populations (children, elderly, terminally ill)

### WARNING Violations
- Unsubstantiated supplement efficacy claims
- Undisclosed paid partnerships for health products
- Vague "detox" or "cleanse" claims
- Weight loss claims without scientific backing
- Using misleading credentials or authority
- Cherry-picking scientific evidence
- Before/after photos without context or typical results

### INFORMATIONAL Notices
- Missing "not medical advice" disclaimers
- Structure/function claims missing FDA disclaimer
- Using "may help" or "supports" without citations
- Generalizing personal experience as universal advice
- Minor credential misrepresentations
